## Stage 2: Two Buses Connected by a Line

{/* Synopsis: Adding a second bus and a transmission line between them introduces impedance, voltage angles, and the physics of power flow. Power doesn't take the "shortest path" — it distributes according to Kirchhoff's laws and line impedance. This stage shows why AC and DC power flow formulations exist and when each is appropriate. */}

## Stage 3: Meshed Network

{/* Synopsis: With three or more buses and multiple paths between them, power flow becomes non-trivial. Parallel paths mean that adding a line can paradoxically increase flow on existing lines (Braess's paradox). Power Transfer Distribution Factors (PTDFs) quantify how injection changes at one bus redistribute across all lines — the key to understanding congestion management. */}
{/* Key concept: parallel paths, PTDFs, loop flow */}
{/* Diagram: stage-3_meshed-network.svg — Three+ bus nodes forming a loop, multiple lines, generators and loads distributed */}

## Stage 4: Economic Dispatch and OPF

{/* Synopsis: Given multiple generators with different cost curves, economic dispatch finds the cheapest way to meet demand. Optimal Power Flow (OPF) adds network constraints — line limits, voltage bounds — to the dispatch problem. The result is Locational Marginal Prices (LMPs) that reflect both generation cost and delivery congestion. This is where power systems become optimization problems. */}
{/* Key concept: cost curves, merit order, OPF, LMP */}

{/* New elements: cost curve annotations, LMP values at buses */}

## Stage 5: Transmission Limits and Congestion

{/* Synopsis: When a transmission line reaches its thermal limit, it creates congestion — the price separation between buses that drives the entire market for transmission rights. Congestion is not a failure; it's the normal state of a stressed grid. LMP decomposition into energy, loss, and congestion components reveals where value is created and destroyed. */}

## Stage 6: Contingency Analysis and SCOPF

{/* Synopsis: Real grids must survive the unexpected loss of any single element (the N-1 criterion). Security-Constrained OPF (SCOPF) solves the dispatch problem while ensuring the grid remains feasible under every credible contingency. This is the most computationally intensive problem in power systems — and the ultimate test of a modeling tool's capability. */}
